Five things I learned deploying Claude across a business

After nearly a decade of leading large teams at Google, I took a CTO role because I wanted to build again. Here's what I found.

1

The vendor evaluation matters more than you think

We ran structured pilots across every major AI platform before selecting Claude. Most vendors demo well. The differences show up when you put them into production workflows with real data and real users. Output quality, safety behaviour, and API design separated Claude from the field. But the decision that mattered most was one I didn't expect: which platform's approach to safety would my board actually trust?

2

Build infrastructure, not training programs

My instinct was to start with training. Teach everyone how to prompt. Run workshops. That's backwards. I built a company-wide data lake that ingested every business system and connected Claude-powered agents to automate the reports people already needed. Finance didn't have to learn prompting. They just got better reports, faster. When people see AI solving their actual problems, adoption follows naturally.

We got quoted $100K+ and a team of 5-6 engineers for that data lake. I built it in weeks with Claude.

Two months in, I got the hardest feedback of the rollout: slow down. Acting on it is what got us to 100% adoption.
3

Your pace is not the team's pace

This was the hardest lesson. I was so focused on building capability that I didn't prioritise the human side. Two months in, I got direct feedback: the pace of change was overwhelming people. That was uncomfortable to hear, but it was the most important input I received. I slowed down, shifted to a people-first approach, and invested in department-specific training and hands-on support. That pivot is what got us to 100% adoption. Not the technology. The trust.

4

Governance is an accelerant, not a brake

We chose to slow down deployment to get our AI usage policy right before scaling: no customer-facing content without human review, no PII in prompts, structured oversight for every department. It felt costly at the time. But it built the trust that made everything else possible. I saw the same pattern leading Google Cloud's largest government contract: the hardest questions were never about performance. They were about governance, sovereignty, and risk.

5

The real product is the conversation

After nine months on the buyer's side, I've learned that what enterprise customers need isn't a better demo. They need someone who has been where they are. Someone who can say: here's what the evaluation process actually looks like, here's where the resistance comes from, here's how to build the board-level business case, and here's what governance should look like before you scale. The board wants to talk about risk and governance. The executive team wants growth and efficiency. The VP of Engineering wants to know if it will actually work with their stack. I've sat in every one of those seats, so I know what each audience needs to hear. I've had those conversations as the buyer. I want to have them as the seller.

Perspective

What I see in the Australian market

After 20 years selling technology across every major Australian vertical, and now deploying AI as a buyer, here's where I see the opportunity.

Most enterprises are stuck between experimentation and deployment

The Australian market is full of companies that have run a proof of concept or have a handful of people using AI informally. Very few have made the leap to enterprise-wide deployment. The gap isn't capability or budget. It's that nobody internally has done it before. They don't know what the evaluation process should look like, how to build the business case, or how to handle the change management.

Safety will be the deciding factor for regulated industries

BFSI, government, healthcare, energy. These verticals aren't choosing the model with the best benchmark scores. They're choosing the platform their compliance team will approve and their board will trust. Having advised the Governance Institute of Australia on AI governance, I know these buyers. Their first question is never "how fast is it?" It's "how do we govern it?" Anthropic has a better answer to that question than anyone in the market.

The mid-market is underserved and ready

The conversation about enterprise AI is dominated by the ASX 20. But the companies between 100 and 5,000 employees are where adoption will move fastest. They have fewer legacy systems, shorter procurement cycles, and leadership teams that can make decisions in weeks instead of quarters. I've seen this firsthand: we went from evaluation to 100% deployment in four months. These businesses don't need a 12-month pilot. They need someone who's done it at their scale and can show them how.
